Jón Ásgeirsson / Halldór K. Laxness

Analysis by Nanna Katrín Hannesdóttir


Warning: this is about love.

The poem Maístjarnan by Halldór Laxness in combination with Jón Ásgeirsson‘s song should be well known to most. It‘s apparent political message and fighting spirit have made it somewhat of an anthem for the labor class‘s struggle for equality in Iceland. However, on reading the Laxness novel it is in, I discovered that Maístjarnan is actually, at it‘s core, a love song more than anything else. This I wanted to share with you.

The poem Maístjarnan is from the novel Heimsljós or world-light which was released in 1937-38 (originaly in two or more volumes).

It is about the search for light and beauty in extremely harsh conditions. The main character is the poet, Ólafur Kárason, and the book is his life‘s story. Ólafur also has an extra name „ljósvíkingur“ or light-viking.

Despite being called light-viking, Ólafur is no soldier in the traditional sense. In fact, quite the opposite. He hates all conflict and has within him the strongest will to constantly please others. This often gets him into trouble. Things are always „just happening“ to him, totally by accident.

In the part where Maístjarnan is, for example, Ólafur finds himself „by accident“ engaged to a woman he does not like at all. They have all of a sudden been together five years and life is a constant struggle. They are extremely poor, have had two children- one who died and one who is sick-to die. The reader gets the feeling that Ólafur is in a self-made prison because no matter how much he wants it deep inside, he would never have the guts to leave this woman.

But in the prelude to Maístjarnan some winds of change have started to blow. There is turbulance in the small town where they live. Workers have started to think about demanding better wages and a labor union is being formed for the first time.

Our Ólafur runs into the emerging labor union‘s first meeting (accidently though, ofcourse) and meets a young woman, Jóa, who has just moved to town. They talk and instantly have a connection and Ólafur feels sparks of infatuation for the first time in a long time. Jóa and Óli bond through shared dreams and she says to him „it is strange to hear from someone else, your own most beautiful thoughts“. And so another sort of turbulance starts, in Ólafur Kárasons personal life.

Despite their deep connection Óli and Jóa have a hard time getting on the same page. He is, ofcourse, enganged and not willing to threaten the fragile peace of his household. Jóa means trouble, and Ólafur does not want any drama so he often tries to just avoid her. When they do meet they don‘t even understand each other so well for they are very different. Jóa is very passionate and political and active in the class struggles going on- a true fighter. She is always asking Ólafur to take a stance, is he for or against the labor union? Ólafur, however, is a dreamer and he prefers not to get too involved in the toil of the real world. He refuses as he can to take a stance in the debate and just wants to keep the peace.

That is up until the day that Maístjarnan was written. Then there is a big demonstration. Ólafur is in the crowd and looks up at Jóa who is standing on a stage with the speakers. At this point all his shields of indifference go down and he describes how he „suddenly he sees her completely as she is“-And she is, in his eyes, the Living image of freedom. He falls completely in love with her and comes home a changed man. He has gotten a spark of hope that his life could change for the better. He sees Jóa as his gateway to freedom, his savior.

While laying in bed that night he sees one small star in the sky. He feels the star comes down to meet him and hears a song forming in his heart a song about her, the girl he calls the Living image of freedom. And we have Maístjarnan:

Ó hve létt er þitt skóhljóð
ó hve leingi ég beið þín,
það er vorhret á glugga,
napur vindur sem hvín,
en ég veit eina stjörnu,
eina stjörnu sem skín,
og nú loks ertu komin,
þú ert komin til mín.

Það eru erfiðir tímar,
það er atvinnuþref,
ég hef ekkert að bjóða,
ekki ögn sem ég gef,
nema von mína og líf mitt
hvort ég vaki eða sef,
þetta eitt sem þú gafst mér
það er alt sem ég hef.

En í kvöld lýkur vetri
sérhvers vinnandi manns,
og á morgun skín maísól,
það er maísólin hans,
það er maísólin okkar,
okkar einíngarbands,
fyrir þér ber ég fána
þessa framtíðarlands.


The first verse is quite subtle and sweet, the star has arrived to give him power.

The second verse moves into a dramatic description of the hard times going on both in Ólafurs life and society.

The last verse then brings with it the golden promise of something better. The little star in the first verse has grown to become a sun. Ofcourse all suns are stars, but when talking about „sun“ we are usually referring to our own sun. In that sense the impersonal and abstract star of hope has become more personalized and tangible in the last verse.

The last two lines when he talks about „fyrir þér ber ég fána, þessa framtíðalands“ are also interesting. In the demonstration Jóa is carrying this red flag upon the stage. Then, there bursts out a riot and in the midst of caos Ólafur offers to carry the flag for her. Jóa tells him no, not for me but for someone else. But in the poem ofcourse Ólafur gets to carry it.

In the end Maístjarnan is a poem about hope. Whether or not one wants to see it as political hope, the hope of new love or just something else.

For enthusiasts the poem should be roughly at this location:

Heimsljós - Hús skáldsins, chapter 15, page 121
