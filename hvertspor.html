Analysis by Nanna Katrín Hannesdóttir


Hvert örstutt spor is from a little bit different context than our previous poems. It is from a play, Silfurtúnglið, which premiered in 1954 (about 15-20 years later than Heimsljós). In the later years of his career Laxness expiremented with writing plays along with novels, none of which were as great of a success as the earlier pieces though.

Jón Nordal, our songs composer, made a song to Hvert örstutt spor specially for the premiere of the play and that same song has lived on ever since. Hvert örstutt spor is thus not a poem turned into a song later but a song from the very start. What is more it is very central in the play and appears multiple times, it sort of binds the storyline together.

I will not go into great depths with the story this time- because then I would definitely spoil too much. But in short the story is about a young mother Lóa, who lives in the countryside in Iceland. The play begins with a scene in Lóa’s home where she is singing Hvert örstutt spor to her little baby boy as a lullaby. An old friend of Lóa’s who is now a world famous singer has just arrived in town for a visit and accidentally overhears the singing. The friend is mesmerized by the beauty and purity of the lyrics and song and l contacts a friend of hers, the shady agent/manager Feilan O Feilan, who owns a theatre in Reykjavík called Silfurtúnglið. Long story short this Feilan becomes very interested in making an act out of Lóa singing Hvert örstutt spor to be performed in Silfurtúnglið. Lóa is hesitant but curious and more than a little flattered and eventually decides to leave her small town and her baby boy and heads to the capital….

If you just look at the song, Hvert örstutt spor itself it’s easy to understand why the characters of Silfurtúnglið were so captivated by it. The lyrics are beautiful. It is a poem full of gratitude and humility; a declaration of a mother’s love to her little baby. In the first and second verse she describes how happy she is, now that he is in her life, and how empty the joy she felt before is in comparison with her love towards him. She describes the wonder of just getting to hearing his voice. -There is a lot of wonder in this poem which gives it so much extra depth. She is always asking; how can something as wonderful as my baby boy exist? It is like she gets a new perspective on life- In the third verse she points out how innocent someone who has not seen the cruelty of the world is in a quite unforgettable way. The fourth and last verse is then just pure gratitude. Pure god -or the bigger picture, or the cosmos or whatever (if you don’t find the thought of god beautiful). She is grateful to get to experience these precious moments, but points out dramatically in the end that they are fleeting.

Hvert örstutt spor var auðnuspor með þér,
hvert andartak er tafðir þú hjá mér
var sólskinsstund og sæludraumur hár,
minn sáttmáli við guð um þúsund ár.

Hvað jafnast á við andardráttinn þinn?
Hve öll sú gleði er fyrr naut hugur minn,
er orðin hljómlaus utangátta og tóm
hjá undrinu að heyra þennan róm.

Hjá undri því að líta lítinn fót
í litlum skóm og vita að heimsins grjót,
svo hart og sárt er honum fjarri enn
og heimsins ráð sem brugga vondir menn.

Já, vita eitthvað anda hér á jörð
er ofar standi minni þakkargjörð,
í stundareilífð eina sumarnótt,
ó alheimsljós, ó mynd sem hverfur skjótt.


A fun fact to end with (coming from my grandmother). In 1955, following the premier of the play a club called Silfurtunglið was opened on the second floor of Austurbæjarbíó (where the árshátíð was). This club was open about 20 years. And what is more, the company that is renting out Austurbær now calls one of it’s rooms Silfurtunglið. It’s probably the same one as where the club was; on the second floor. So not the one we sung in, but almost!

Nanna Katrín Hannesdóttir
